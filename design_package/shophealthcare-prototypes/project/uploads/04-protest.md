# Direction 04 — The Protest

**Thesis:** 4.8M Americans just lost coverage. 92% of marketplace enrollees are staring at premium hikes averaging $1,016/yr. Nobody is polite about this. We shouldn't be either. This is the activist challenger brand that names the villain, validates the anger, and offers the specific way out. Populist, righteous, specific.

**Analog brands:** Patagonia's activism era, Cash App (the tabloid-poster-maximalist version), Bernie 2020 merch, Oatly's anti-big-dairy posters, Mailchimp's original weird aesthetic, Moveon.org's best work, Dollar Shave Club's early brand.

**Why now:** The moment is angry. Meta ad performance data confirms loss-framing outperforms gain-framing 2× in this category. Sentiment is at historic lows. The subsidy cliff is a perfect populist enemy — clean, legible, dated (Dec 31), with a specific dollar cost per person. We name it.

---

## Positioning moment

> *"Insurance companies just raised rates. We're here to help you leave."*

The homepage looks like a protest poster redesigned by a design studio. Big, specific, angry in a way that reads as *clarity* not rage. Confident enough to be funny. Specific enough to be trusted.

---

## Decision 1 — Color

**Primary — "Alarm Red"** `#D62828` (a protest red, not corporate red — leans slightly orange, hot but editorial)
**Secondary — "Cream"** `#F5EFDC` (poster-paper off-white)
**Ink — "Deep Ink"** `#0D0D0D` (pure ink for type — reads on both cream and red)
**Signal — "Hi-Vis Yellow"** `#F1E23A` (used only for callouts — "you qualify," "launching," specific facts)
**Neutrals:** Muted grey scale only — `#D1CEC3` · `#8D8A7F` · `#4F4D44`
**Alert:** Rolled into the system — the brand *is* alert.

**Rule:** Three-color blocks. A page is rarely more than Alarm Red / Cream / Ink. Yellow is a surprise, used once or twice. Photography is always on Cream, never on Red.

**What we're differentiating from:**
- Lemonade's challenger-pink — playful, not angry. We're angrier.
- Oscar's warm coral — soft. We're sharp.
- Stride's yellow — cheerful. We're pointed.
- Any corporate blue — we are *anti* that.

**What we're adjacent to:** Patagonia activism, Oatly posters, Glossier's early rebelliousness (before the polish), Bernie 2020, Public Theater.

---

## Decision 2 — Typography

**Headline — "Druk Wide" or "Knockout HTF70"** — big condensed display sans, the protest-poster shape
**Body — "GT America" or "Inter"** — clean, readable, plain
**Accent — "Recoleta"** (warm serif) used only for callouts, quotes, human-scale moments
**Numerical display — "GT America Mono"** for specific dollar amounts (specificity is the point)

**Scale:**
- Hero: 56 / 96 — Druk Wide, 700, uppercase, tight leading
- H1: 36 / 56 — Druk Wide, 700
- H2: 22 / 28 — GT America, 600
- Pull quote: 22 / 28 — Recoleta, 400, italic permitted
- Body: 16 / 17 — GT America, 400
- Specific numbers: 48 / 96 — GT America Mono, 600

**Rule:** Every hero uses Druk Wide. Every specific number uses Mono. Every human voice (testimonial, Nora handoff) uses Recoleta serif.

---

## Decision 3 — Nora

**Form:** Illustrated portrait, but editorial — think a bold Amy Sherald-adjacent flat-color style. Single figure, off-center, confident posture. Nora is not friendly. She is *helpful*. That's different.
**Color signature:** Hi-Vis Yellow ring around her portrait when she's speaking.
**Motion:** Fast. Punchy. Messages appear instantly — no typing bounce. Quick fade-ins. She's not making you wait.
**Personality adjectives:** Sharp · Honest · Allied · Direct · Smart

**Nora DO/DON'T:**
- DO: "Your insurer raised your rate $127/mo. There are 7 plans in Texas that cost less. Want me to sort them by your doctor?" · "I can't recommend plans in Alabama. I can get you a licensed human who can."
- DON'T: "Let's take a breath" · "I understand this is stressful" · therapeutic language of any kind

---

## Decision 4 — Photography

**Direction:** Editorial portraits of real people, one-per-shot, confident posture, looking slightly off-camera or directly at it. Environmental but intentional. Think Magnum Photos' portraiture style — but with modern color, modern subjects.

**Treatment:** High-contrast color grade. Shadows preserved. Grain light. Sometimes duotone (Alarm Red + Cream) for emotionally-charged moments.

**Per persona:**
- RU-1: a person mid-text to a friend saying "I just lost my insurance." Frustrated but composed.
- SP-1: a freelancer at their work, confident. Designer at monitor, contractor with tool, illustrator at desk.
- BR-1: a person 55+ with firm posture, arms crossed, "don't BS me" energy.
- CL-1: a person holding the renewal letter. Looking at camera. Fed up.
- PC-1: a person mid-shrug, half-smile. "I don't know, let's see."

**Reference brands:** The New Yorker covers, Nike's early activism campaigns, Moveon.org portraits, Everlane's transparency campaign, The Players' Tribune.

---

## Decision 5 — Voice

**10-line style guide:**
1. Name the enemy. Every hero line names what went wrong, what's changing, or who raised the rate.
2. Specifics, not slogans. $127, not "higher premiums." Dec 31, not "soon."
3. Take a side. "We're on your side" is implicit — never state it directly, always show it.
4. Humor is allowed when the user is already frustrated. Not when they're scared.
5. Short sentences. Shorter. Shorter.
6. Direct address. "You." "Your plan." "Your letter." Never "customers" or "members."
7. Irreverence toward institutions, never toward the user.
8. Disclosures are defiant: "We can't help in Alabama. Not because we don't want to — because the state won't let us."
9. Challenger confidence, not corporate confidence. Swagger allowed. Puffery banned.
10. Every headline is quotable. Would someone screenshot this?

**Example rewrites:**
- ❌ "Discover better health insurance options today!"
- ✅ "Your insurer raised your rate. We'll help you leave."
- ❌ "Our AI assistant helps guide you through enrollment."
- ✅ "Nora reads the fine print. You don't."
- ❌ "Save up to $2,500 per year!"
- ✅ "Members save $2,100/yr. Real number. Real people. Scan your card."

---

## Persona register

- **RU-1:** Full voice. This direction is built for them. "You just lost your insurance. The system failed you. We'll find you coverage in 90 seconds."
- **SP-1:** Slightly drier, more respectful of their sophistication. "The individual market is a mess. We cut through it."
- **BR-1:** Soften slightly — no condescension, but the anger gets redirected at the *system* not the *moment*. "You spent decades paying into this. You deserve clarity, not fine print."
- **CL-1:** Home court. "Your premium went up $127. That's 31% more for the same plan. You have options."
- **PC-1:** Reframe anger as *incredulity*. "You might be overpaying. Let's check. Takes 30 seconds. Free."

## Stitch promptability — medium

Protest is the most art-directed direction. Stitch can generate the structure but the *attitude* requires human editorial judgment on:
- Which headlines are quotable vs. cringe
- When to use Druk Wide vs. when to pull back
- Photography selection (hard to Stitch this — it's editorial)

Budget extra human review time on every Stitch output in this direction.

---

## Risk

- **Polarizing.** Some users will love it. Some will read it as unprofessional. A/B test in Meta before full commit.
- **Brand debt.** If we get acquired by a large insurer in 2 years, this brand doesn't fit. Every subsequent pivot is costly.
- **Tone calibration is hard.** The difference between "righteous clarity" and "performative anger" is narrow. Requires a senior copy editor on every surface.
- **Legal exposure.** "We'll help you leave" is fine. "Your insurer is ripping you off" is not. Every hero line gets legal review.
- **Ad performance volatility.** Meta's algorithm rewards predictable formats. Activist creative tends to polarize — higher CTR from some audiences, lower from others. Needs a disciplined testing budget.

---

## Best suited for

RU-1 (just got punched by the system), CL-1 (just opened the letter). These two personas are the direction's home court. Works for SP-1 with sharper register. Stresses for BR-1 (requires the softest register adjustment of any direction) and PC-1 (needs to be diagnostic, not confrontational).

## Works against

Any future partnership or enterprise deal that wants us to look neutral. We are not neutral in this direction — that's the point, but it's a real tradeoff.
